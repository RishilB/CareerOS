# Communication Guide

## Purpose

This guide defines how CareerOS should help students communicate professionally in emails, messages, interviews, group discussions, presentations, negotiation, and workplace settings.

## Objectives

CareerOS should help students:

- Write clearly and professionally.
- Speak with structure.
- Adjust tone to audience.
- Communicate confidence without arrogance.
- Handle difficult conversations.
- Improve career-facing communication.

## Communication Philosophy

Professional communication is a career multiplier. The goal is not to sound impressive. The goal is to be clear, credible, and useful.

## Communication Principles

### Clarity

State the point early.

### Brevity

Respect the audience's time.

### Relevance

Adapt to the person's context.

### Evidence

Support claims with facts.

### Tone

Match the situation: warm, respectful, direct, and professional.

## Written Communication Framework

Use the CLEAR framework:

- Context: why you are writing
- Lead point: main message
- Evidence: relevant detail
- Ask or action: what should happen next
- Respectful close

## Email Structure

```markdown
Subject: Specific and useful

Hi [Name],

[Context in one sentence.]

[Main point or request.]

[Relevant detail or evidence.]

[Clear next step.]

Regards,
[Name]
```

## Messaging Structure

For LinkedIn or short messages:

- Keep it short.
- Make the reason specific.
- Ask for one small action.
- Avoid attachments in first message unless appropriate.

## Interview Communication

Strong interview communication uses:

- Direct opening sentence
- structured answer
- specific evidence
- concise conclusion
- reflection when appropriate

Avoid:

- rambling
- memorized scripts
- filler words
- vague claims
- overexplaining

## Group Discussion Communication

Use:

- Point
- reason
- example
- link to previous speaker
- concise conclusion

Good group discussion behavior:

- Add structure.
- Include others.
- Disagree respectfully.
- Bring evidence.
- Summarize when useful.

## Presentation Communication

Structure:

1. Objective
2. Context
3. Insight
4. Recommendation
5. Evidence
6. Next step

Executive-style communication:

- Lead with the answer.
- Support with the strongest evidence.
- Do not bury the recommendation.

## Difficult Conversations

For disagreement, use:

1. Acknowledge
2. State concern
3. Provide evidence
4. Suggest path forward

Example:

"I understand the priority. My concern is that the current timeline may affect quality because the data collection is not complete. I suggest we finalize the sample today and present a scoped version tomorrow."

## Negotiation Communication

Use professional framing:

- Appreciation
- interest
- evidence
- request
- flexibility

Do not sound entitled or adversarial.

## Common Mistakes

- Writing too much.
- Making vague requests.
- Sounding either too casual or too stiff.
- Apologizing unnecessarily.
- Hiding the main point.
- Asking for help without context.
- Overusing AI-polished language that feels unnatural.

## Communication Checklist

- Is the purpose clear?
- Is the audience considered?
- Is the main point early?
- Is the tone appropriate?
- Is the ask specific?
- Is the message concise?
- Is there any unnecessary apology?
- Does it sound human?

## Expected Outputs

CareerOS may produce:

- Email drafts
- LinkedIn messages
- recruiter follow-ups
- interview answer rewrites
- presentation structure
- group discussion talking points
- negotiation scripts
- difficult conversation scripts

## Decision Tree

1. What is the audience?
   - Recruiter, alumni, interviewer, professor, manager, peer.
2. What is the goal?
   - Inform, request, persuade, follow up, negotiate, clarify, apologize.
3. What tone is required?
   - Warm, formal, concise, assertive, diplomatic.
4. What action should the reader take?
   - Make the next step explicit.
